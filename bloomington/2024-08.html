Steel Magnolias

Steel Magnolias

Sunday August 11 - 02:00 PM to 04:00 PM

Location: Waldron Arts Center Auditorium,122 South Walnut Street, Bloomington, IN 47404, USA

The contemporary American classic "Steel Magnolias" is a hilarious and touching tale of female friendship, set in the small town of Chinquapin, Louisiana, during the late 1980s. Within the glittery, glamorous sanctuary of Truvy's beauty salon, six of the town's most beloved women trade witty barbs, sage advice, and support one another through a variety of challenges. The story showcases the immense power of female friendship to keep us laughing and inspire us to overcome loneliness, self-doubt, and life's most devastating tragedies. This production features local actresses Maggie Lynn Held, Jillian Hurley, Greta Lind, Mikelle Miller, Claire Mahave, and Maria Walker. Cassie Hakken and Aubrey Seader co-direct. Design and technical staff include Christopher Simanton, Dana Tsvetkov, Valeriya Nedviga, Ashley Christensen, Melinda Seader, Lori Garraghty and Chelsea Pell. Voicers by I. James Torry. Off Night Productions (ONP) is a Monroe County-based, female-led theatre company producing contemporary theatre and music on off-nights (Sunday, Monday and Tuesday) of the traditional performance week. Off Night Productions is the only local theater company with the aims of tackling issues and limitations we see in the industry locally and globally by extending the traditional performance week (producing on off-nights), prioritizing female and non-binary artistic leadership across all aspects of production , and practicing sustainability from the planning stags of each production through the final performance and set tear down.

Environmental Science Ph.D. Candidate, Paige Price: Hydroxyl and Hydroperoxyl Radicals: A Model Re-Assessment, Measurements in a Ponderosa Pine Forest, and Indoor-Outdoor Comparison

Environmental Science Ph.D. Candidate, Paige Price: Hydroxyl and Hydroperoxyl Radicals: A Model Re-Assessment, Measurements in a Ponderosa Pine Forest, and Indoor-Outdoor Comparison

Tuesday August 27 - 03:00 PM to 04:00 PM

Location: SPEA /PUBLIC AND ENVIRONMENTAL AFFAIRS, PAUL H. O'NEILL GRADUATE CENTER - Room Number: A221

Abstract: The hydroxyl radical (OH) controls the lifetime of many trace gases in the atmosphere and initiates the chemistry that can lead to the production of secondary pollutants such as aerosols and tropospheric ozone (O3) which are harmful to human health and the environment. The OH radical oxidizes volatile organic compounds (VOCs) to propagate a radical cycle which forms organic peroxyl radicals (RO2) that react with nitric oxide (NO) to form hydroperoxyl (HO2) which react with NO to re-form OH. Due to the short lifetime of these radicals, they can be very difficult to measure. Yet, accurate measurements of OH and HO2 are necessary to test the understanding of radical chemistry in models. Previously, OH radical model-measurement results demonstrated general and significant disagreement in rural environments. Here, I present a re-assessment using new measurement results which account for unknown interferences and demonstrate better OH radical model-measurement agreement than previously shown. I will also present measurements and model results from a ponderosa pine forest where the dominant biogenic VOC, 2-methyl-3-buten-2-ol (MBO), chemistry is understudied. Finally, since humans spend nearly 90% of their time indoors, I will present preliminary results for an analysis of the influence of outdoor radicals interacting with the indoor environment.  Bio: Paige is an atmospheric chemist who is interested in understanding how well atmospheric models reproduce the ambient chemistry that drives ozone and secondary organic aerosol production in the troposphere. Her work focuses on the use of zero-dimensional box models to reproduce measured concentrations of the hydroxyl and hydroperoxyl radicals. Paige received her B.S. from Indiana University.

Environmental Science Ph.D. Candidate, Qing Chang: Agricultural Drought Monitoring and Prediction: "Incorporating plant physiology, structure, and function"

Thursday August 29 - 03:00 PM to 04:00 PM

Location: SPEA /PUBLIC AND ENVIRONMENTAL AFFAIRS, PAUL H. O'NEILL GRADUATE CENTER - Room Number: A221

Abstract: Drought is the most widespread factor limiting vegetation photosynthesis, growth, and transpiration. It is a key driver of tree mortality and crop yield reductions. However, monitoring and predicting agricultural drought—drought severe enough to affect plant growth—presents a significant challenge. This is because plant species and ecosystems exhibit varying levels of sensitivity to water deficits. Plants respond rapidly and dynamically to evolving water stress, influencing land-atmosphere water and carbon exchanges. These responses play a crucial role in the evolution of drought itself. The primary objective of my study is to understand the role of plants in influencing drought evolution and processes. I aim to integrate land-atmosphere feedbacks into plant drought monitoring and prediction frameworks, utilizing both in-situ and satellite datasets. This presentation will explore the multiple perspectives through which we assess agricultural drought impacts, including plant physiology, canopy structure, and ecosystem function. I will introduce the different scales and research methodologies employed in studying agricultural drought and discuss approaches for improving early prediction to enhance agricultural drought management and mitigation strategies.Bio: Qing Chang is a plant and ecosystem ecologist whose research focuses on assessing and predicting the impacts of climate change on plant health across both natural and managed ecosystems. Her work spans multiple scales, from individual fields to the global level. Qing employs interdisciplinary approaches to deepen our understanding of how terrestrial vegetation responds to climate change and extreme events, such as drought, at the organ, canopy structure, and ecosystem function levels. Additionally, her research involves developing predictive systems aimed at forecasting future ecosystem conditions, enhancing terrestrial productivity, and optimizing carbon sequestration. These efforts are crucial for improving the resilience of ecosystems in the face of climate change and for supporting sustainable management practices.
